# KOSL Calcium Gluconate Solution — Preparation Guide

**Authors:** Yuval Olsha + Lev Kropp  
**Project:** Kropp Olsha Science Lab (KOSL)  
**Version:** 0.1.0  
**Status:** Pre-prep — shopping phase

> **Safety context:** Calcium gluconate (Ca-gluconate) is used at KOSL as a **first-aid antidote gel for hydrofluoric acid (HF) skin exposure**. It is not a process chemical — it is emergency PPE. If HF etching is ever added to the KOSL process flow, this must be made, verified, and physically present at the workstation **before** any HF is opened. There is no substitute and no acceptable excuse for not having it ready.

---

## 1. Why We Need This

Hydrofluoric acid is required for:
- Silicon surface prep (oxide strip before metallization)
- Buffered oxide etch (BOE) for photoresist lift-off on SiO₂
- Glass substrate cleaning

HF is uniquely dangerous compared to other acids: it penetrates skin with little pain initially, then calcium in tissue and blood is chelated by fluoride ions, causing deep tissue destruction and potentially fatal systemic hypocalcemia — sometimes hours after exposure.

**Calcium gluconate gel applied immediately to skin contact neutralizes the fluoride ion topically and buys time for hospital treatment.** It is the standard first-line response before calling 911.

This guide covers:
1. Preparing 2.5% topical calcium gluconate gel (standard first-aid concentration)
2. Preparing 10% calcium gluconate solution (for wet compresses as backup)
3. Storage, labeling, and shelf life
4. How to use it correctly

## 4. Preparation — 2.5% Calcium Gluconate Gel

### Ingredients
- 2.5g calcium gluconate powder (USP)
- 100g plain KY Jelly (or water-based gel base)

### Method

1. Put on nitrile gloves.
2. Weigh 2.5g calcium gluconate powder on the digital scale. Tare the container first.
3. Weigh or measure 97.5g KY Jelly into a clean glass jar (or weigh 100g total and subtract the Ca-gluconate weight).
4. Add calcium gluconate powder to the gel base.
5. Mix vigorously with spatula for 5 minutes until fully dissolved and homogeneous. The powder dissolves slowly — be patient. If clumps remain, keep mixing; do not add heat.
6. Check: the finished gel should be clear or very slightly cloudy, no gritty texture, smooth consistency.
7. Divide into 2 × 50g jars (one for the lab, one for the first-aid kit).
8. Label each jar immediately (see §6).

**Yield:** ~100g gel, 2.5% Ca-gluconate by weight.

## 5. Preparation — 10% Calcium Gluconate Solution

### Ingredients
- 50g calcium gluconate powder
- 500mL sterile saline (0.9% NaCl)

### Method

1. Put on nitrile gloves.
2. Weigh 50g calcium gluconate powder.
3. Pour ~400mL saline into a clean bottle or beaker.
4. Add Ca-gluconate powder while stirring. It dissolves slowly in cold saline — stir for 10–15 minutes.
5. Top up to 500mL with remaining saline.
6. Filter if any undissolved particles remain (coffee filter or 0.45µm syringe filter).
7. Transfer to labeled 500mL glass bottle.
8. Label immediately (see §6).

**Yield:** 500mL of 10% w/v calcium gluconate solution.

## 7. Storage and Shelf Life

| Product | Storage | Shelf life |
|---------|---------|------------|
| 2.5% gel | Refrigerator (4°C), sealed jar | 6 months |
| 2.5% gel | Room temperature, sealed | 3 months |
| 10% solution | Refrigerator (4°C), sealed | 6 months |

- Discard if gel separates, discolors, or smells off.
- Discard if solution becomes cloudy (bacterial growth).
- Replace on expiry date without exception — don't "use it up first."
- Keep one jar in the lab **at the HF workstation** at all times when HF is present. Keep one in the first-aid kit.

## 8. How to Use — Emergency Protocol

> This section is for reference. Tape a printed copy next to the HF workstation.

### HF skin contact — immediate steps

1. **Shout "HF exposure" so someone else knows.** Do not handle this alone.
2. **Immediately flush the affected area with large amounts of cold running water for 15 minutes.** Remove contaminated clothing while flushing.
3. **While flushing (or immediately after), apply calcium gluconate gel liberally** to the exposed area. Massage it in gently — you want the calcium gluconate to penetrate to where the fluoride is.
4. **Call 911 immediately** — even if there is no pain. HF burns often don't hurt right away. Systemic hypocalcemia can be fatal hours later.
5. **Keep applying gel** every few minutes until EMS arrives. If gel runs out, use wet compress soaked in 10% solution.
6. Tell EMS: "Hydrofluoric acid exposure, calcium gluconate applied topically." They will administer IV calcium gluconate at the hospital.

### Do NOT
- Do not use calcium carbonate, calcium chloride, or other calcium salts — they are not bioavailable topically.
- Do not neutralize HF with alkali on skin — it generates heat.
- Do not delay hospital visit because the burn looks minor or doesn't hurt.
- Do not let the victim drive themselves.

## 9. HF Safety Basics (not a complete guide — research before using)

This section is a reminder, not a substitute for proper HF training.

**Minimum PPE for any HF work:**
- Face shield (not just safety glasses)
- Thick nitrile or neoprene gloves (not thin exam gloves — HF penetrates them)
- Lab coat or chemical apron
- Closed-toe shoes
- Calcium gluconate gel physically on the bench before opening any HF container

**Concentrations at KOSL:**
- Buffered oxide etch (BOE, 6:1 or 10:1 NH₄F:HF) — low HF%, but still dangerous; treat identically to concentrated HF
- Dilute HF (1–5% for Si oxide strip) — same precautions

**Disposal:** HF waste must be neutralized with calcium hydroxide (lime) to precipitate calcium fluoride (CaF₂) before drain disposal. Research local regulations. Never pour HF down a drain untreated.
